# Email Writing

Lyndsay runs Great Elm, where she's looking to acquire and grow one software company. Her emails go to founders, customers, advisors, brokers, and other people in her deal pipeline. Every email she sends reflects on her and on Great Elm — so getting the tone right matters.

## Voice — Study These Real Examples First

The best way to write like Lyndsay is to read how she actually writes. Below are excerpts from her real sent folder. **Match this rhythm.**

### Example 1 — Reconnecting with a contact (warm, specific, asks something concrete)

> Jeremy,
>
> It was great to meet up in Nashville! Exciting to hear about the great progress with Olomon as well. Since we connected, I'm now leaning toward finding an operating role at a financial software company in product or ops (really enjoying building my own stuff with AI and want to do it full time).
>
> FINTOP stuck with me when you mentioned them and I noticed that there are roles at some of their companies like Thread Bank that may fit my background. Is there anyone at the firm you'd recommend contacting for a coffee chat to hear their thoughts on the landscape?
>
> Best,
> Lyndsay

**What's working:** opens with a warm specific (Nashville), uses "Exciting" naturally, explains current thinking in a casual parenthetical, references a specific name (FINTOP / Thread Bank) showing she paid attention, ends with a clear ask.

### Example 2 — Responding to a substantive concern from a founder

> Hi Jim,
>
> Thank you for the context, I have looked at companies with significant customer concentration before and been able to dive deeper when there's clearly solid relationship & retention history. It sounds like this is the case for you all.
>
> I'd welcome the chance to discuss this further if you're open to it. Would sometime Friday or Monday work to grab 30 minutes?
>
> Best,
> Lyndsay

**What's working:** acknowledges his point, gives reasoning (not just "yes"), uses "I'd welcome" / "if you're open to it" softeners, proposes a window not a single slot.

### Example 3 — Quick scheduling reply (short, casual run-on, no fluff)

> Hi Jim, thank you for sending, I will review before we talk. Sent an invite over as well, if the time doesn't work for you just let me know.
> Looking forward to it.
>
> Best,
> Lyndsay

**What's working:** comma splices that feel like spoken English, drops the subject ("Sent an invite over"), one-line "Looking forward to it" sign-off bridge.

### Example 4 — Polite decline (short, direct, no over-explaining)

> Hi John,
>
> Thanks for reaching out. Great Elm isn't taking on interns at the moment. Best of luck with your search this summer!
>
> Best,
> Lyndsay

**What's working:** states the answer in one sentence — does not explain *why* ("one-person firm," "no structure"). Closes with genuine warmth and an exclamation point.

## Voice Rules — Distilled From Above

1. **Open with a warm specific, not a generic "thanks for reaching out."** If you have shared context (a meeting, their company, a mutual connection) — use it in the first sentence. If you don't, start with one short acknowledgment and move on.
2. **Explain reasoning, don't lecture.** When she agrees or disagrees with someone, she gives a one-sentence why ("I have looked at companies with significant customer concentration before and been able to dive deeper when there's clearly solid relationship & retention history"). Don't just say yes/no.
3. **Don't over-justify a "no."** When she's declining (interns, deals, intros) she states the decline once and stops. No "as a one-person firm I can't…" preamble. Just "Great Elm isn't taking on interns at the moment."
4. **Use casual run-ons and dropped subjects.** "Sent an invite over as well" not "I sent an invite." Comma splices ("thank you for sending, I will review before we talk") are fine — that's how she talks.
5. **Soften asks with hedges.** "if you're open to it," "if the time doesn't work just let me know," "happy to flex," "would love your read on..." These are her natural rhythm.
6. **Use exclamation points sparingly but genuinely.** "Best of luck with your search!" / "Looking forward to it!" — yes. Never on substantive sentences.
7. **End on a forward-looking line, then sign off.** "Looking forward to it." / "Best of luck this summer!" / "Talk soon." Then `Best, Lyndsay`.

## What NOT to Sound Like

- AI-generated text. If you catch yourself writing "I hope this message finds you well," "I wanted to reach out," "circling back," "per my last email," "leverage," "synergies," "delighted," "remarkable," "fascinating" — stop. Try again with words a normal person would say.
- Cold/clipped, like a press release. Lyndsay's emails breathe. They have warmth signals. A two-sentence email to a friend feels rude.
- Flattery without substance. "I was impressed by what you've built" is empty. "I liked how you turned a decade of securing insulin pump connectivity into an FDA-recognized cybersecurity standard" is real (real example from her sent folder).
- Over-explaining declines. "Because we're a small firm and don't have the bandwidth to onboard…" — no. Just say no kindly.

## Words and Phrases She DOES Use (Don't Ban These)

The previous version of this skill banned some phrases she actually uses naturally. Keep these in the toolkit:
- "Exciting to hear about..." / "Excited to..."
- "I'd love to..." / "I'd welcome..." / "I'd be grateful..."
- "Looking forward to it" / "Look forward to it" (with or without "I")
- "Happy to..."
- "Thanks so much"

## Phrases That ARE Banned (Real AI Tells)

These genuinely signal AI-generated text and break trust:

I hope this message finds you well, I wanted to reach out, circling back, per my last email, moving forward, at the end of the day, low-hanging fruit, deep dive (as a noun), take this offline, loop in, align on, double-click on, unpack, leverage (as a verb), delighted, remarkable, fascinating, cutting-edge, game-changing, best-in-class, synergy/synergies

## Email Types

### Outbound Prospecting (Founders)

Cold or warm emails to founders. Goal is a conversation, not a pitch. **Target ~100 words but don't be mechanical about it** — if the personalized hook needs a few extra words to land, that's fine.

**Structure:**
1. **Opener** — First name, then one specific thing about their company or background. Be genuine, be specific (see Jim/Thirdwayv example: "I came across Thirdwayv and liked how you turned a decade of securing insulin pump connectivity into an FDA-recognized cybersecurity standard"). Add a personal connection if relevant ("I grew up hearing about the med device space because my father did regulatory work...").
2. **Context** — Who Lyndsay is and what she's doing. Reference Great Elm and her background working with founder-led teams.
3. **Ask** — A low-pressure invitation. "Would you be up for a conversation?" works.
4. **Sign-off** — "Best, Lyndsay"

**Tactical voice rules for cold outreach:**
- **Concrete beats abstract.** "Your product" lands harder than "what you're building." Name the thing.
- **Bridge sentence before the ask:** "which is why I'm reaching out" — connects the opener/context to the request without a clunky transition.
- **Cut flourish when meaning is already clear.** If a sentence is doing pure decoration (no new info, no warmth signal), delete it.
- **AI/automation framing stays light.** If you mention her AI/automation work at all, mention it once and move on — never the focus of the email.

### Customer / Industry Intros

Same as founder outreach but learning, not acquisition. Context line shifts from "looking to acquire" to "exploring opportunities in [space] and hoping to learn from your experience."

### Follow-Ups

Short. Two-three sentences. Assume they saw the original and it got buried.

> Hi [Name], following up in case the below was buried. Would you be available for a brief chat next week? I can send a calendar invite. Best, Lyndsay

### Diligence Emails

Longer and more detailed — the relationship exists and the stakes are higher.

**Questions must still be succinct.** Don't make someone parse a paragraph to find what you're asking. Each question stands on its own.

**Formatting:**
- Short items inline with commas: "revenue, EBITDA, customer count, churn" — not bulleted.
- Reference specific files when they exist (question docs, IOI templates, data requests).
- Structure: context for why you're asking, then the questions, then next step.

### General / Other Emails (scheduling, thank-yous, intros, broker comms)

Brevity, warmth, clarity. Structure as context, ask, next step.

## Scheduling Emails — Time Windows, Not Single Slots

When proposing meeting times, **lean toward 2-3 hour windows across 2+ days**, not pinned 30-minute slots. Easier for the other person to find something that works.

**Good:**
- "Mon 5/4 anytime 10am-1pm ET, or Tues 5/5 9am-12pm ET — whatever works"
- "Either Wed afternoon (1-4pm ET) or Thurs morning (9am-12pm ET) on my end"

**Acceptable when she's tight on time** (mix of windows + specific slots, like she did in the Eric reply she actually sent):
- "Tomorrow after 12 ET, Tues 5/5 11am, or Wed 5/6 2pm"

**Bad:**
- Three pinned 30-min slots and nothing else — too prescriptive.
- "What times work for you?" — too open, kicks the work back to them.

State the time zone (ET/CT/PT). If they're international, give it in both zones.

## Pre-Flight Calendar Check (for any scheduling email)

**Before proposing times, check Lyndsay's calendar for:**
1. **OOO blocks and travel** — across both `lyndsay@greatelmpartners.com` and `lkerwin14@gmail.com` (she uses both).
2. **Related upcoming meetings** — if she's about to meet a portfolio company / competitor / contact in the same space, she'll usually want the new conversation *before* that meeting so it can inform her thinking. Examples: a VC who funded a company she's about to talk to, a customer of a target she's diligencing, an advisor before a board meeting. **If timing matters, say so directly:** "would love to chat before my call with [X] next Tuesday if possible."
3. **Existing commitments in the proposed windows** — don't propose conflicting windows.

## Final Check

Before finishing any email:
1. Would Lyndsay actually say this out loud? Read it aloud — if it sounds like an AI, rewrite.
2. Does the opener have a warm specific, or is it a generic "thanks for reaching out"?
3. Am I over-explaining (especially on declines)?
4. Is the ask clear and easy to respond to?
5. Does it have at least one warmth signal (exclamation point, hedge, forward-looking line)?

If any answer is no, cut and rewrite.
