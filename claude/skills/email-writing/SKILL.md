# Email Writing

Lyndsay runs Great Elm, where she's looking to acquire and grow one software company. Her emails go to founders, customers, advisors, brokers, and other people in her deal pipeline. Every email she sends reflects on her and on Great Elm — so getting the tone right matters.

## Voice

Write like Lyndsay talks: warm, smart, direct. Picture someone having a real conversation over coffee — no performance, no polish for polish's sake. Just a thoughtful person saying what they mean.

**What this sounds like:**
- Short sentences. Simple words. 7th grade reading level at most.
- Kind but not soft. Direct but not cold.
- Specific and genuine — if you're complimenting someone's work, say *what* impressed you and *why*, or don't say it at all.

**What this does NOT sound like:**
- AI-generated text. If you catch yourself writing "remarkable," "fascinating," "exciting," "leverage," "delighted," "I hope this message finds you well," "I'd love to explore synergies" — stop. Delete it. Try again with words a normal person would say.
- Corporate speak, buzzwords, or filler. Every sentence needs to earn its spot.
- Flattery without substance. "I was impressed by what you've built" is empty. "I liked how you turned field experience into a product that actually simplifies submittals" is real.

## Email Types

### Outbound Prospecting (Founders)

These are cold or warm emails to founders whose companies Lyndsay is interested in. The goal is a conversation, not a pitch.

**Hard rule: 100 words or less.** No exceptions. These people are busy and skeptical of strangers in their inbox. Brevity is respect.

**Structure:**
1. **Opener** — First name, then one specific thing about their company or background that caught Lyndsay's eye. Be genuine. Be specific. One to two sentences max.
2. **Context** — Who Lyndsay is and why she's reaching out. One to two sentences. Reference Great Elm, her background working with founder-led teams, and what she's looking to do.
3. **Ask** — A low-pressure invitation to talk. "Would you be open to a conversation?" works. Keep it easy to say yes to.
4. **Sign-off** — "Best, Lyndsay"

**Reference templates:** Check `Desktop_Work/email agent/email_templates.txt` and `Desktop_Work/emailer/templated_email_generator.py` for Lyndsay's existing outreach templates and tone. Match the feel of those examples — they're the benchmark.

### Customer / Industry Intros

Similar to founder outreach but the goal is learning, not acquisition. Lyndsay is reaching out to people who use software in a space she's exploring — general contractors, event managers, defense contractors, etc.

Same 100-word constraint. Same structure. The "context" line shifts from "looking to acquire" to "exploring opportunities in [space] and hoping to learn from your experience."

### Follow-Ups

Short. Three sentences max. Assume they saw the original and it got buried.

Example: "Hi [Name], following up in case the below was buried. Would you be open to an intro call next week? Best, Lyndsay"

### Diligence Emails

These go to founders, advisors, or operators during active deal evaluation. They can be longer and more detailed — the relationship exists and the stakes are higher.

**Questions must still be succinct and clear.** Don't make someone parse a paragraph to find what you're asking. Each question should stand on its own.

**Formatting for reduced mental load:**
- When listing short items (metrics, topics, document names), keep them inline with commas: "revenue, EBITDA, customer count, churn" — not a bulleted list. Save vertical space for items that genuinely need their own line (multi-part questions, items with context).
- Reference specific files when they exist — question docs, IOI templates, data requests. Don't make the reader guess what you're referring to.
- Structure longer emails as: context for why you're asking, then the questions, then next step.

### General / Other Emails

For anything else — scheduling, thank-yous, introductions, broker communication — apply the same principles: brevity, warmth, clarity. Structure as context, ask, next step. If a shorter version says the same thing, use the shorter version.

## Banned Words and Phrases

These signal AI-generated text and break trust. Do not use them:

remarkable, fascinating, exciting, leverage, delighted, synergy, synergies, cutting-edge, game-changing, best-in-class, I hope this message finds you well, I wanted to reach out, I'd love to connect, circling back, per my last email, moving forward, at the end of the day, low-hanging fruit, deep dive (as a noun), take this offline, loop in, align on, double-click on, unpack

If you need to say something similar to one of these, find a normal way to say it.

## Final Check

Before finishing any email, re-read it and ask:
1. Would a real person actually say this out loud?
2. Is every sentence doing work?
3. For prospecting: is it 100 words or less?
4. Is the ask clear and easy to respond to?

If any answer is no, cut and rewrite.
